# NABARD Study Report: Adoption of Micro-Irrigation in Western India

Document ID: NABARD-MI-ADOPTION
Issuing authority: National Bank for Agriculture and Rural Development (NABARD), Department of Economic Analysis and Research
Report type: State-of-sector analytical report

## 1. Scope

This report analyses the adoption of micro-irrigation (drip and sprinkler) across major western Indian States and estimates State-level adoption indices relative to a national baseline. The indices are used by lenders and planners to gauge relative penetration and headroom for growth.

## 2. Adoption index (baseline: national average = 1.00)

The adoption index measures the ratio of a State's micro-irrigation penetration (area under micro-irrigation as a share of net sown area) against the national average. A value above 1.00 indicates above-average penetration.

- Gujarat: 1.42
- Andhra Pradesh: 1.30
- Maharashtra: 1.00 (used as the study's regional reference)
- Karnataka: 0.95
- Rajasthan: 0.88
- Madhya Pradesh: 0.74

Gujarat's high index is attributed to the early establishment of a single-window nodal agency and the "new adopter" incentive that lowered the entry barrier for first-time users. Maharashtra's penetration is concentrated in horticulture belts, with lower penetration in cereal-dominated districts.

## 3. Drivers of adoption

The report identifies four principal drivers of drip adoption: (1) the effective subsidy rate available to the farmer, (2) the simplicity of the application and disbursement process, (3) the price of the water-intensive alternative (flood irrigation) including electricity and water costs, and (4) the presence of high-value horticulture or cash crops that make the investment economically attractive.

## 4. Water savings and yield

Field studies summarised in the report indicate that drip irrigation reduces water use by 40% to 60% compared with flood irrigation for horticulture crops, and raises yields by 20% to 50% depending on crop and management. For sugarcane specifically, drip has been observed to reduce water application by up to 50% while improving cane yield and sucrose recovery.

## 5. Financing gap

Despite subsidies, the report notes that the farmer's own contribution (25% to 45% of system cost depending on State and category) remains a barrier for marginal farmers. NABARD recommends bundling micro-irrigation with Kisan Credit Card (KCC) refinance and FPO-level aggregation to reduce the effective upfront burden on individual farmers.

## 6. Recommendation on subsidy design

The report observes that above a threshold, higher headline subsidy percentages produce diminishing returns on adoption; process simplicity and reliable, timely disbursement matter more at the margin than the last few percentage points of subsidy. States with faster disbursement showed higher repeat-adoption rates even where their headline subsidy was lower.
