# Technical Guide: Design and Installation of Drip Irrigation Systems

Document ID: DRIP-TECH-GUIDE
Issuing authority: Compiled technical reference (extension guidance)
Document type: Technical / engineering guide

## 1. Principle

Drip irrigation (also called trickle irrigation) delivers water slowly and directly to the root zone of plants through a network of pipes, valves, and emitters. Because water is applied at or near the point of use, conveyance and evaporation losses are minimised and application efficiency typically reaches 85% to 95%, compared with 40% to 60% for surface (flood) irrigation.

## 2. Main components

A drip system consists of:

- Water source and pumping unit: borewell, well, or surface source with a pump sized to the required discharge and pressure.
- Head control unit: comprising a non-return valve, a bypass, filters, a fertigation device, pressure gauges, and an air-release valve.
- Filtration: screen filters for clean water, sand (media) filters for surface water with organic load, and hydrocyclone (sand separator) filters where the water carries suspended sand.
- Mainline and sub-mains: usually rigid PVC pipes buried below ground that carry water from the head unit to the field.
- Laterals: flexible polyethylene (LDPE/LLDPE) pipes running along crop rows, carrying emitters.
- Emitters (drippers): devices that discharge water at a controlled low rate, commonly 2, 4, or 8 litres per hour. Emitters may be online (fixed onto the lateral) or inline (built into the lateral at fixed spacing).

## 3. Emitter selection and spacing

Emitter discharge and spacing are chosen based on soil type and crop. Sandy soils, where water moves downward faster than sideways, need closely spaced emitters or higher discharge to wet a continuous strip. Clayey soils, where lateral movement is greater, tolerate wider spacing. For widely spaced orchard crops, one or more emitters are placed per plant; for closely spaced row crops, inline laterals with regular emitter spacing are used.

## 4. Filtration is critical

Emitter clogging is the most common cause of drip-system failure. Clogging may be physical (suspended particles), chemical (precipitation of salts, especially calcium carbonate and iron), or biological (algae and bacterial slime). Correct filtration matched to the water quality, periodic flushing of laterals, and acid or chlorine treatment where required are essential to keep emitters functioning.

## 5. Fertigation

Fertigation is the application of soluble fertilizers through the irrigation water. A venturi injector or a fertilizer tank introduces nutrients into the system so they are delivered directly to the root zone. Fertigation improves fertilizer use efficiency and allows precise, split application of nutrients through the crop cycle. Only fully water-soluble fertilizers should be used to avoid clogging.

## 6. Maintenance schedule

Routine maintenance includes: flushing of mainlines and laterals at regular intervals, cleaning of filters (daily during peak season for media filters), checking emitter discharge uniformity, and periodic chemical treatment to prevent scaling and biological growth. A well-maintained drip system has an operational life of 8 to 10 years for laterals and longer for buried mains.

## 7. Common design errors

Frequent errors include undersized filtration, excessive lateral length causing pressure loss and non-uniform discharge along the row, mismatched emitter discharge for the soil type, and omission of pressure-regulating devices on undulating terrain. Pressure variation across the field should be kept within acceptable limits so that emission uniformity remains high.
